Javier Sevilla Salcedo

I'm Javier Sevilla Salcedo, and I am currently diving into the world of research and teaching at the Systems Engineering and Automation Department at Carlos III University of Madrid. My academic journey began with a B.Sc. in Industrial Electronics and Automation Engineering from the University of Jaén, which opened up a path to a master's in Robotics and Automation right back at Carlos III University.

I'm currently pursuing my Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering, Electronics, and Automation, which is pretty exciting. Along the way, I've focused on theories and put what I learned into practice. I've had the fantastic opportunity to work in the robotics and automation field within the automotive industry, getting a taste of how these concepts are applied in the real world.

I've also devoted over four years to some fascinating research areas—automating processes, refining robotics, exploring how robots can interact socially, and diving into Natural Language Processing. Part of my hands-on experience was designing a robotic guitar tuner for my bachelor's project. For my master's thesis, I developed NLP applications that help make social robots more engaging and intuitive.

My current research interests are pretty broad, with a strong focus on Natural Language Processing. But I'm also passionate about Social Robotics, Deep Learning, and Deep Reinforcement Learning, as well as how robots cognitively perceive and interact with the world.

Publications

GERT: Transformers for Co-speech Gesture Prediction in Social Robots

GERT (Gesture-Enhanced Robotics Transformer), a set of three transformer-based models designed to predict gestures that align with a robot's verbal communication. The research evaluates and compares the effectiveness and efficiency of the three model variations, demonstrating that they all perform well (with F-scores ranging from 0.78 to 0.86) while highlighting the balance between model size and performance.

Using Large Language Models to Shape Social Robots’ Speech

Two methods are proposed: one for rephrasing existing utterances to retain their meaning but introduce variation, and the other for creating topic-specific speech that adapts to the conversation partner. The results are promising, but challenges such as computational demands, interaction delays, reliance on proprietary models, and the need for subjective evaluation are noted as constraints.

Predicción de gestos no-verbales usando aprendizaje profundo

Robots are starting to be used in social settings and need to autonomously execute non-verbal gestures to interact naturally with people, synchronizing them with speech. This study introduces a system that employs transformer models, a recent advancement in deep learning, to predict social robots' non-verbal gestures. This system is compared with a previous one that used recurrent networks and conditional random fields, showing that transformers significantly enhance the prediction of non-verbal gestures for social robots.

User-Adapted Semantic Description Generation Using Natural Language Models

This work examines the use of natural language generation models to improve the fluidity of human-computer conversation by creating a system that generates user-profile-specific descriptions on a variety of topics. This approach moves away from using predefined scripts in conversational agents, aiming to make interactions more natural and friendly. The applications were implemented and showed that integrating these models in conversational agents is highly effective.

Using Deep Learning for Implementing Paraphrasing in a Social Robot

Social robotics focuses on creating robots that can engage in natural interactions with humans, such as conversing in a human-like manner. A study has introduced deep learning models that enable a robot to produce varied yet semantically consistent paraphrases in Spanish, enhancing its conversational abilities. These capabilities have been incorporated into the Mini robot, which can operate in both English and Spanish and function either locally or on a server. Evaluation of three models—the T5 and Pegasus Transformers, as well as a fine-tuned version of T5 (Finetuned T5)—along with Parrot and a fine-tuned Pegasus for generating paraphrases, revealed that the Finetuned T5 yields less diverse but more accurate paraphrases and is faster by about one second.

ARTETERAPIA: una estrategia para incrementar el vínculo en la robótica social

The advancement of technology and an aging population have created a societal divide between the elderly and the young. Social robotics has the potential to narrow this gap by bringing technology closer to older adults. Despite its potential to assist and reduce loneliness among the elderly, social robotics isn't widely adopted. The article proposes the development of tools to strengthen human-robot interaction and suggests art therapy with social robots to enhance the psychophysical state of the elderly. This intervention aims to maximize the functionality and well-being of older adults while fostering a stronger bond between them and the robots, providing motivation for continued engagement with the technology.

Modelos de lenguaje natural para robots sociales

Robots are increasingly becoming part of our daily lives, particularly in social contexts where they interact with humans and undertake various tasks. For these social robots to communicate effectively and appear friendly, their speech should not rely on repetitive predefined texts. The paper discusses how advancements in natural language generation models can enhance robot communication, making human-robot interactions more fluid and paving the way for new opportunities and applications within robotics. The implementation of these models has shown promising potential for integrating natural language generation in robotics.

A Bio-Inspired Endogenous Attention-Based Architecture for a Social Robot

This paper introduces a novel perception architecture for a social robot, inspired by biological mechanisms of endogenous attention, which enriches the robot's understanding of its environment by integrating multiple sensory inputs. The architecture is designed both theoretically and practically for insight into bio-inspired processes and for actual implementation in a robot's system. It includes mechanisms to identify the most important stimuli and is linked with the robot's decision-making system to enable responses to the environment and user interactions. Preliminary test results from deploying this architecture in a real social robot are also presented.
