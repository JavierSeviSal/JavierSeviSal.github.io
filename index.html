Javier Sevilla Salcedo

I'm Javier Sevilla Salcedo, and I am currently diving into the world of research and teaching at the Systems Engineering and Automation Department at Carlos III University of Madrid. My academic journey began with a B.Sc. in Industrial Electronics and Automation Engineering from the University of Jaén, which opened up a path to a master's in Robotics and Automation right back at Carlos III University.

I'm currently pursuing my Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering, Electronics, and Automation, which is pretty exciting. Along the way, I've focused on theories and put what I learned into practice. I've had the fantastic opportunity to work in the robotics and automation field within the automotive industry, getting a taste of how these concepts are applied in the real world.

I've also devoted over four years to some fascinating research areas—automating processes, refining robotics, exploring how robots can interact socially, and diving into Natural Language Processing. Part of my hands-on experience was designing a robotic guitar tuner for my bachelor's project. For my master's thesis, I developed NLP applications that help make social robots more engaging and intuitive.

My current research interests are pretty broad, with a strong focus on Natural Language Processing. But I'm also passionate about Social Robotics, Deep Learning, and Deep Reinforcement Learning, as well as how robots cognitively perceive and interact with the world.

Publications

GERT: Transformers for Co-speech Gesture Prediction in Social Robots

GERT (Gesture-Enhanced Robotics Transformer), a set of three transformer-based models designed to predict gestures that align with a robot's verbal communication. The research evaluates and compares the effectiveness and efficiency of the three model variations, demonstrating that they all perform well (with F-scores ranging from 0.78 to 0.86) while highlighting the balance between model size and performance.

Using Large Language Models to Shape Social Robots’ Speech

Two methods are proposed: one for rephrasing existing utterances to retain their meaning but introduce variation, and the other for creating topic-specific speech that adapts to the conversation partner. The results are promising, but challenges such as computational demands, interaction delays, reliance on proprietary models, and the need for subjective evaluation are noted as constraints.

Predicción de gestos no-verbales usando aprendizaje profundo

Robots are starting to be used in social settings and need to autonomously execute non-verbal gestures to interact naturally with people, synchronizing them with speech. This study introduces a system that employs transformer models, a recent advancement in deep learning, to predict social robots' non-verbal gestures. This system is compared with a previous one that used recurrent networks and conditional random fields, showing that transformers significantly enhance the prediction of non-verbal gestures for social robots.
